Reporting for the Country with Scientific Research Strength and Promoting the National Strength with Scientific and Technological Innovation

With the development of astronomy in China, it will further promote China's economic and social development and better serve the production and life of the broad masses of the people.
Astronomy has always been a symbol of the strength and civilization of a country. China's astronomy has had a glorious past. China has the world's oldest and richest records of solar eclipses, comets and other astronomical phenomena, and exquisite ancient astronomical instruments such as turbidity instrument and simplified instrument.
After the founding of New China, the Chinese astronomical community began to catch up with the world's advanced level. Especially with the reform and opening up, China's astronomy has ushered in a golden age of development.
In the 21st century, China's astronomy has made remarkable achievements.
【Li Heng · Chengdu】
Li Heng has been engaged in teaching and Astronomy Research for a long time. He has edited Cosmos and Astronomical Journal. He has written Statistical Studies of Textual Variable Stars, Models of Red Giant Stars, Photographic Studies of Five Galactic Clusters, etc. He attached great importance to popular science all his life. His speeches were lively and interesting, and his works were rich and colorful. Since the 1920s, he has published a large number of popular science articles in magazines such as Science, such as The Development of Amateur Astronomy and Scientific Outlook on Life. Before the founding of the People's Republic of China, Li Heng was a researcher of the Institute of Astronomy of the Academy of Central Research, and after the founding of the People's Republic of China, he served as president and honorary director of the Shanghai Observatory of the Chinese Academy of Sciences. He has contributed to the creation and development of modern astronomy in China and enjoyed a high reputation in the astronomical circles at home and abroad.

Wang Yongli: China's Sun Exploration Satellite will be launched in 2021
The sun, the nearest star to us, influences the earth's alternation of day and night, the change of seasons and the growth of all things. What is the significance of exploring and studying the sun for human beings?

Wang Jingxiu: We live in the solar system and are dominated by the sun, so the changes of the sun not only affect the environment of the earth, the living conditions of human beings, but also the whole solar system and its planetary system.

(In addition) nowadays, human beings rely more on high technology, such as satellite technology, communication technology, long-distance transmission, oil transmission, navigation and positioning, which may be threatened by intense solar activity. Intense solar activity can interrupt short-wave communication, or the navigation and positioning can not be carried out correctly, and even lead to the failure of scientific satellites to work properly or even fall.

Therefore, in today's high-tech, the intense activity of the sun and the resulting disastrous weather should be one of the important natural disasters facing mankind.

Why on earth does the solar system thrive? Where are the neighbors outside the human solar system? We need to think about exoplanet systems that can migrate, but how do you find them? The sun and solar system provide us with guidance.

So in these sense, the study of the sun is closely related to the human living environment and the human high-tech system.

It is so important to explore the sun. Does China have its own plan for exploring the sun? How is progress?

Wang Jingxiu: Our advanced space-based Solar Observatory project has been approved by the state formally, and has already started to work.

The Advanced Space-based Solar Observatory (ASSO) is a solar space exploration satellite program spontaneously proposed by the solar physics community in China. It will be the first time to simultaneously observe the solar all-solar vector magnetic field, solar flare high-energy radiation imaging and the near-heliospheric propagation of coronal mass ejection on a satellite. It will reveal the intrinsic physical mechanism of solar flare eruption and coronal mass ejection caused by the evolution of solar magnetic field, which will not only expand the field of human knowledge, but also have a serious impact. The space weather of human living environment provides the physical basis for forecasting.

Our main scientific objective is to answer two of the most important forms of solar activity: flares and coronal mass ejections. To explore the relationship between solar flares, coronal mass ejections and solar magnetic fields.

Building China's VLBI Network

In 1973, Ye Shuhua boldly proposed to develop very long baseline interferometry (VLBI) technology in China. In January 1986, under the leadership of Ye Shuhua, the Shanghai Observatory completed the "Proposal for the Development of China's VLBI Network" and planned the outline of China's VLBI Network: the new Urumqi VLBI Station, the reconstruction of Kunming 10-meter antenna to VLBI Station, the upgrading of Shanghai VLBI Data Processing Center, etc. The proposal was recommended by the Astronomical Committee, and the Chinese Academy of Sciences agreed to set up a project, which was designated as a major project during the Seventh Five-Year Plan period of Tiankou. The project was named the second phase of the VLBI network project, and Ye Shuhua, the chief engineer of the project, was established. It is her unremitting efforts and strategic vision that made the Shanghai Observatory and the Chinese astronomy rapidly catch up with the trend of international astronomy from classical observation to space observation in the 1980s and 1990s. People who have worked with Ye Shuhua say that her eyes are always far away, but she says, "Opportunity is right in front of you. How can we not seize it?"

Time, Time

Where was the earliest Beijing Time we used every day? The world's two most precise time systems, one in France, consisting of 39 observatories, and the other in the former Soviet Union, supported by 17 observatories. But the astronomical observatories that we could use at that time were not enough for other people's spare parts. The Zijin Mountain in Nanjing and Xujiahui in Shanghai were all.

Ye Shuhua led the team to test and calibrate repeatedly to overcome the difficult problem of mathematical model. He added astronomical time to the National Astronomical institutes for six years and established the world time of independent measurement in China. Popularly speaking, the Beijing time we use today is the result of the calibration of Ye Shuhua's team at that time.

This year, at the age of 32, she led China to rank second in the world in the accuracy of world time measurement. Years later, she became the director of the Observatory. She was invited to France for exchange. On her parting, foreign colleagues toasted her: "Cheers to the director of the Observatory."
